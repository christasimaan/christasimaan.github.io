I conducted CIS research on the Formalization of Permutation Networks through Penn Undergraduate Research Mentoring (PURM) Program and part of the Deep Specification research team.
Permutation Networks are circuits of configurable switches, such that their output is always a permutation of the input. Our choice of permutation network was the Benes network. In order to prove and work with permutation networks, we used a proof-based programming language called Coq. We created circuitry and we used abstraction in the creation to simplify the composition to allow for ease in future use.
We had around 600 lines of definitions in Coq. We also had around 400 total lines of proofs, totaling to around 1,000 lines of code. This also gave a 3:2 ratio of code to proofs. We attribute the proof size to using dependent types and our use of Ltac proof automation.

A brief PURM Recap. I also conducted research for the first time on Pascal's Triangle at North Carolina's Governor's School East over the summer. My group and I derived recursive formulae for a pattern we founded called Consecutive Rows with Even Middle Terms (CREMT). We then created a Python program to generate and predict the total percentage of even numbers per row compared to every row before that.
We created a different program where odds were represented by a 1 and evens were 0. We correlated this convetion to the triangle row and generated 1’s and 0’s back. With this formula, we found that every row after a CREMT contains all odd numbers. This was the reason for an exponential growth of even values and exponential decay of odd values throughout the whole, infinite triangle.
